Character Formation

Contrary to popular belief most Chinese characters do not consist of pictographs but form a combination of semantic and phonetic elements. We can distinguish between five types of character formation:

Pictographic characters (3%)
Pictographs are stylized images of the object they depict.
Examples:
日rìsun 人rénman 木mùtree
羊yángsheep 山shanmountain 口kǒumouth
Pictophonetic characters (80%+)
Pictophentic characters consist of a pictograph which hints at the meaning of the character and a phonetic element that indicates the character's pronunciation:
方fāng, square)+户 (hù, door)=房(fáng, house)
Indicative characters (1%)
The character represents an abstract meaning graphically:
上shàngabove 下xiàbelow 三sānthree
Associative characters (13%)
The meanings of the component are combined into a new meaning:
明 (ming, 日, rì, sun + 月, yuè, moon) = bright
安 (ān, 宀, mián, roof + 女, nǚ, woman) = peace
Borrowed characters (2%)
A character is used to represent a s similar sounding syllable. The original meaning is lost, that is , it is replaced by the new meaning.
Example:
自 (zì, self) originally meaning 'nose'. (The currently used character for nose is 鼻, bì)
