Chinese Calender

The traditional Chinese calendar 农历 (nónglì) is based on the moon and the sun.
The Chinese year runs from one Chinese new year (新年) to the next.
The start of the new year falls between 21 January and 20 February, normally on the second new moon after the winter solstice.

Important festivals in the Chinese year are:
Month 1, Day 1: 春节 (chūnjié) Spring Festival
(Chinese New Year)
Month 1, Day 15: 元宵节 (yuánxiāojié) Lantern Festival
105 days after winther solstice: 清明节 (qīngmíngjié) Tomb Sweeping Festival
Month 5, Day 5: 端午节 (duānwǔjié) Dragon Boat Festival
Month 8, Day 15: 中秋节 (zhōngqiūjié) Mid Autumn Festival
There are several ways of naming a year:
  • Traditionally most years were counted as years in the reign of the then current emperor (sometimes counting from important events within a reign). After the collapse of the Qing dynasty the count started with the 1st year of the Republic (e.g. 1912 = 1. Year of the Republic). The People's Republic of China adopted the Western (Gregorian) calendar while Taiwan still officially counts the number of years since 1912.
  • A countinous count starting from 2697 or 2637 BC (the reign of 黄帝).
  • A continous cycle of 60 years made up of a ten and a twelve year cycle (the ten heavenly stems and twelve earthly branches).
The earthly branches also take the names of animals:
